Day 1Quick Win
Stop calling yourself a videographer

Hey [FIRST NAME],

Quick one today.

The single biggest positioning mistake filmmakers make is calling themselves a "videographer" or "video editor" or "content creator." Those words tell the client you are a commodity. Someone they can find on Fiverr for $200.

Instead, position yourself as a creative partner. A strategic visual partner. Someone who understands their brand and creates content that drives business results.

The shift sounds small. It is not. When a client sees "videographer," they think cost. When they see "creative partner," they think investment.

Update your bio. Update your proposals. Update how you introduce yourself on calls. Watch what happens.

Day 3Framework
The pitch changes everything

Hey [FIRST NAME],

The difference between a $500 gig and a $10K project is not the work. It is the pitch.

Most filmmakers pitch by sending a price list. Maybe a PDF. Maybe a DM with their rate. The client compares it to every other quote they received. You lose on price because you made it about price.

Premium filmmakers pitch differently. They frame the project as a business investment. They show the client what the content will DO for their brand, not just what it will LOOK like.

I built a framework for this called the 3 Day Pitch Pack. It is a system for building pitches that communicate premium value before the client ever sees your reel. When the pitch is right, the price takes care of itself.

More on this in the next email.

Day 5Story
How I stopped shooting everything for everyone

Hey [FIRST NAME],

When I started, I said yes to everything. Weddings, events, corporate videos, social media clips. Whatever paid. I was 17, traveling, and just wanted to shoot.

The problem with saying yes to everything is that you become known for nothing. No one refers you because they cannot describe what you do.

The turning point was when I started saying no. I picked a lane. I focused on a specific type of client and a specific type of work. I raised my prices. And the weird thing happened: I got more inquiries, not fewer.

When you narrow down, you stand out. Clients do not want a generalist. They want someone who understands their specific world.

That is how PhotoBomb Media started. Not by doing everything. By doing one thing really well for a specific type of client.

Day 7Education
The $500 vs $10K framework

Hey [FIRST NAME],

Two filmmakers walk into the same meeting. Same gear. Same reel quality. Same city.

Filmmaker A sends a one-page quote. "$1,500 for a brand video. Includes filming, editing, and revisions." The client says "let me think about it" and ghosts.

Filmmaker B sends a pitch deck. It opens with the client's business challenge. It shows how the video content will solve that challenge. It includes examples of similar work with measurable results. The price is $8,000. The client says "when can we start?"

Same meeting. Same skill level. 5x the price. The difference is that Filmmaker A made it about the video. Filmmaker B made it about the outcome.

Clients do not pay for video. They pay for what video does for their business. When you frame your pitch around outcomes, the price becomes irrelevant.

Jack

Day 10Education
How to stop starting from zero every month

Hey [FIRST NAME],

The freelance cycle is brutal. Finish a project. Get paid. Start from zero. Hunt for the next one. Repeat.

There is a way out: retainer deals.

Instead of selling one-off projects, you structure an ongoing relationship. The client pays monthly. You deliver a set amount of content. Both sides win.

Here is how to pitch it: after your first project together, say this. "I loved working on this. Based on what I know about your brand now, I think we could do a lot more together. Would you be open to a monthly content partnership where I handle [X] for you on a recurring basis?"

Most clients will at least listen. 3-4 retainer clients at $2K-$5K per month and you have a $10K-$20K/month business without chasing a single new lead.

Jack

Day 13Mindset
Your reel is not the problem

Hey [FIRST NAME],

I know what you are thinking. "If I just had a better reel, I would get better clients."

I used to think the same thing. I would spend hours re-editing my portfolio, adding new projects, tweaking the color grade. I thought that would land the next big client.

It never did.

The filmmakers charging $10K+ per project do not necessarily have better reels. They have a system for finding, pitching, and closing premium clients. The reel is a tool in that system. It is not the system itself.

If your reel is decent (and it probably is), the bottleneck is not your work. It is your client acquisition process.

Fix the process. The reel will do its job once the right people are looking at it.

Jack

Day 16Education
Where premium clients actually hang out

Hey [FIRST NAME],

Most filmmakers look for clients in the wrong places. They post on social media hoping someone will reach out. They browse job boards. They wait for referrals.

Premium clients are not on job boards. Here is where they actually are:

Industry events and conferences. The people making buying decisions for brand content are at events. Show up. Network. Follow up.

LinkedIn. Not with a generic "hey I do video" message. With a targeted pitch that references their specific business challenge.

Through existing clients. Your best source of premium clients is other premium clients. Every project should end with: "Who else do you know who could benefit from this?"

Local business communities. Chambers of commerce, startup meetups, co-working spaces. The businesses there need content and have budget.

Stop waiting for clients to find you. Go where they are.

Jack
