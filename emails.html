The $300 project that changed how I think about money

Welcome to The Modern Filmmaker Blueprint. You just joined 1,000+ filmmakers who are learning how to turn their cameras into real businesses. I am glad you are here.

Day 1Quick Win
The word that is costing you clients

Hey [FIRST NAME],

A few years ago, I was at a networking event in Melbourne. Someone asked me what I did. I said "I am a videographer."

I watched his face go blank. He nodded politely. "Cool. My nephew does videos too." Then he walked away.

That word. Videographer. It killed the conversation before it started. In the client's mind, "videographer" means commodity. It means someone they can find on Airtasker for $150. It means a line item, not a partner.

The next event I went to, I changed one thing. When someone asked what I did, I said: "I help brands tell their story through film. I work with businesses to create content that actually drives customers and revenue."

Same person. Same type of conversation. Completely different reaction. "Oh, interesting. We have been thinking about doing something like that. Can I get your card?"

That single reframe opened more doors than any reel I have ever sent. The work did not change. How I described it did.

Here is what I want you to do today. Go to your Instagram bio, your website header, and your email signature. Replace "videographer" or "video editor" or "content creator" with language that describes the outcome you create for clients. Not what you do. What it does for them.

One sentence. Today. Then watch what happens to the next five conversations you have.

Jack

Day 3Framework
The pitch I built in a hotel room that landed a $12K client

Hey [FIRST NAME],

I was in a hotel room in Sydney. I had a pitch meeting the next morning with a brand I really wanted to work with. Instead of building a standard quote sheet, which is what I always did, I tried something different.

I spent three hours researching their business. I looked at their competitors. I looked at their social content. I found the gaps. Then I built a pitch that was not about me at all. It was about them.

The first slide was not my reel. It was their problem. "Your competitors are outpacing you on video content. Here is what they are doing that you are not." Then I showed specific examples. Then I laid out a plan for how my work would close that gap. The pricing came last, almost as an afterthought.

I walked out with a $12,000 contract. The biggest deal I had closed at that point. And the client barely looked at my reel.

That process became the 3 Day Pitch Pack. The name is literal. In three days, you can research, build, and deliver a pitch that reframes the entire conversation from "how much do you charge" to "when can we start."

The core idea is simple: stop pitching your services. Start pitching the client's outcome. When they see themselves in the pitch, the price becomes secondary.

Tomorrow I will tell you about the time I said no to a $4,000 gig. And why it was the best business decision I ever made.

Jack

Day 5Story
The gig I turned down (and what happened next)

Hey [FIRST NAME],

A client offered me $4,000 to shoot a wedding. At the time, that was serious money. My immediate instinct was to say yes.

But I had just started saying no to projects that did not fit the direction I wanted to go. I wanted to work with brands. Commercial work. Stuff that built my portfolio toward higher-paying, recurring clients. Weddings were one-offs that filled the calendar but did not build the business.

I said no. It was terrifying. $4,000 is $4,000 when you are young and building.

That same week, because my calendar was open, I took a meeting with a local brand that had reached out. I had time to research them properly. I built a real pitch. I showed up prepared. They signed a $6,000 project with a monthly retainer conversation on the table.

If I had taken the wedding, I would have been booked that week. I never would have taken that meeting. I never would have landed the retainer.

Saying no is a business strategy. Every time you say yes to a project that does not fit your direction, you are saying no to one that does. You just do not see the one you missed because you were too busy shooting the wrong thing.

The filmmakers who charge premium rates are not the ones who take everything. They are the ones who are selective enough that when the right project comes, they have the bandwidth and the positioning to land it.

What would you say no to this week if you trusted that something better was coming?

Jack

Day 7Education
Same meeting. Same reel. 10x the price.

Hey [FIRST NAME],

Picture this. Two filmmakers walk into the same office to pitch the same client. Same city. Same gear. Same reel quality. Maybe the same editing software.

Filmmaker A opens their laptop. Pulls up their reel. Plays it for three minutes. Then says: "So for a project like this, my rate is $1,500. That includes a full day of shooting and editing. I can have it to you in two weeks."

The client says: "Thanks. Let me think about it." They never call back.

Filmmaker B does not open their laptop at all. They start by asking the client three questions. What is the biggest challenge your brand is facing right now with content? What would success look like for this project? And what has your audience been responding to recently?

The client talks for 15 minutes. Filmmaker B listens. Takes notes. Then says: "Based on what you just told me, here is what I would recommend." And walks them through a plan that directly addresses the problems the client just described. The price is $10,000. The client says "when can we start" because the plan feels like it was built specifically for them. Because it was.

The difference is not talent. It is not gear. It is not years of experience. It is the frame. Filmmaker A sold a service. Filmmaker B sold a solution. Clients pay 10x for solutions because solutions are connected to outcomes. Services are connected to hours.

Every pitch you send should answer one question for the client: "What does this do for my business?" If your pitch answers "what do you charge per hour," you have already lost.

Jack

Day 10Education
The conversation that ended the freelance cycle

Hey [FIRST NAME],

I was three months into a relationship with a client. We had done two one-off projects together. Both went well. Both were project-based. Scope, price, deliver, done. Back to zero.

After the second project, I did something I had never done before. I sent the client a follow-up email that said: "I have been thinking about your brand since we wrapped. I have three ideas for content that would build on what we just created. Would you be open to a monthly partnership where I handle your video content on a recurring basis?"

The client said yes. We agreed on $3,500 per month. Four deliverables. One strategy call. That single conversation turned a $5,000 one-off relationship into $42,000 a year. Same client. Same work. Different structure.

The secret to retainer deals is timing and framing. You pitch it after a successful project when trust is highest. You frame it around continuity, not cost. "We built something great together. Let's keep building" is a more powerful pitch than "do you want to pay me monthly."

Three retainer clients at $3K-$5K per month is $9K-$15K per month. That is a six-figure business from three relationships. No cold outreach. No job boards. No starting from zero.

The freelance cycle breaks the moment you stop thinking in projects and start thinking in partnerships.

Jack

Day 13Mindset
Stop editing your reel. Start sending pitches.

Hey [FIRST NAME],

I wasted an entire weekend once re-editing my portfolio. New color grade. New music. New transitions. I was convinced this would be the version that finally attracted the big clients.

On Monday, I showed it to a mentor. He watched 30 seconds, closed the laptop, and said: "Your reel is fine. It was fine last month too. How many pitches did you send this week?"

The answer was zero. I had spent 20 hours perfecting something nobody asked for and zero hours doing the thing that actually generates revenue.

That conversation was embarrassing. It was also the most useful business advice I have ever received.

Here is the truth most filmmakers avoid. Your reel is not the bottleneck. Your client acquisition process is. The filmmakers charging $10K+ per project do not have perfect reels. They have a system for finding the right people, pitching them the right way, and closing the deal.

If your reel is decent, and after watching you update it three times, it almost certainly is, then the next hour you spend on it is an hour you are not spending on outreach. And outreach is where the money is.

This week, instead of opening your editing software, send five pitches. Find five businesses that need video content. Research them for 20 minutes each. Send a personalized pitch using the framework from Email 3. Five pitches. That is it.

One of them will respond. That response is worth more than the prettiest reel in the world.

Jack
